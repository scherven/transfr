transfr · design prototype · issue #53

Levels

The levels tab is weird and unusable, and the export isn't why — the data is fine, the framing throws it away. Two proposals to choose between (they're complementary, not rivals): a ribbon that makes the vertical story glanceable on the card, and a floor plan that frames each level on its own route instead of the whole walk's. Every pixel below is drawn from the two committed viz_export fixtures — Berlin Hbf 1→16 and Dortmund Hbf 11→4. Nothing is faked, and the things the data can't support are listed rather than drawn.

01

What the tab does today

Faithfully re-drawn from the real export, at the real canvas size (326×210pt), with the real algorithm: PlanFit frames every level on the whole path's bounding box. That optimises tab-to-tab continuity and spends the entire canvas doing it. Switch fixtures and floors — the numbers under each panel are measured live, not asserted.

Today shipped

Five defects, all visible above

▪
The shared bbox starves every floor. Berlin's L−2 step-off is a 26×14px mark in a 326×210 canvas — 0.5% of it. Dortmund's L−1 is a 5×13px thread. The other 99.5% is void, spent keeping the frame steady between tabs.
▪
The picker offers floors the path never visits. It's built from levelsPresent — the union of levels over all context ways — while the canvas draws only path geometry. Pick Dortmund L−2: a completely blank panel with a lone chip.
▪
The markers are bigger than the route and say the same thing twice. r=12 discs (24px) on a 5×13px thread; Dortmund's two L−1 discs sit 14.1px apart — overlapping — and both read “Level change ↑ L0”. A mark knows the other floor is above; it never knows it's where you came in versus where you go out.
▪
And on the last floor it inverts the instruction. Berlin L+2 is where you arrive and board — today it draws a down-chevron reading “Escalator ↓ L+1”, because an arrival rendered from “this floor's point of view” looks exactly like a departure. It's telling you to go back down at the moment you've finished.
▪
Phantom elevators. WalkConnector.color falls through to Theme.elev — the exact orange the legend labels “Elevator”. Dortmund's three "vertical" transitions are unmapped level changes; today they draw as three elevators that don't exist.
02

Approach A · The level ribbon

One strip, read left to right in walk order. Segment widths track distance; the risers between them are the real transitions, coloured by their real kind. It answers “how far up, how many changes, what do I take, where's the long bit” without a tap — and it fits on the transfer card, where the walk view can't go. What it replaces

Today the card says this, from the same data:

⇅ 4 level changes — level change + stairs.
▪
“4 level changes” is a count, not a shape. Berlin's four are a straight climb from L−2 to L+2; Dortmund's four are down, up, up, down — back where you started. Same sentence, opposite journeys. The ribbon can't hide that.
▪
“level change + stairs” is the phantom-elevator bug in prose. It's literally reading out "vertical". The ribbon names it once, in the grey we use for “we don't know”.
▪
It shows where the walking is. Dortmund's middle L0 run is 161 m of the 289 m — more than half the walk on one floor. The count says nothing; the ribbon puts it under your thumb.
▪
Stepped vs flat is the real fork, and it's a height budget. Measured off the card mock at 250pt wide: flat is a flat 70pt whatever the walk. Stepped grows with the floor count — 116pt for Dortmund's 3 floors, 172pt for Berlin's 5 — because each floor needs its own band. Flat you have to read; stepped you see. Berlin is the argument for stepped; Dortmund is the argument for stepped twice. If the card can't spend 172pt on a 5-floor change, the honest answer is flat on the card and stepped in the walk view.

Honest about the encoding: with widths ∝ distance on, each segment gets a minimum width so a 2.6 m hop stays visible and labelled — the bar lengths are proportional above that floor, not below it. Toggle it off to see pure proportional widths and what they do to Dortmund's last 2.6 m. Risers are fixed-width: a level change is an event, not a distance. Z is level × 4 m nominal, not surveyed elevation — the stepped ribbon's rises are evenly spaced because that's all the data can honestly claim.

03

Approach B · The floor plan

Same fixture, same canvas, three treatments, left to right. Panel 2 is the minimal fix — frame each level on its own route, drive the picker from the path, pair the markers. Panel 3 adds what the export was already carrying and we were throwing away: the context ways the search touched, a ghost of the floors this one connects to, and a scale bar to hold the mental map together when the zoom changes between tabs. Pick Dortmund, then L−2 to watch the blank tab appear in panel 1 and vanish in panels 2–3.

Worth knowing before you sign it off: own-framing helps exactly the floors the shared bbox starves, and does nothing for the one that was already setting it. Dortmund L−1 goes 6.1×, L+1 4.1×, Berlin's step-off and boarding floors 2.6–2.9× — but Dortmund L0 measures 0.9×, because its own route is most of the path bbox, so there was never anything to reclaim. That floor was never the broken one (it's the only one today draws at a usable size). The fix is worth it for the four starved floors, not for a uniform win — and the numbers under each panel say so rather than hiding it.

Why panel 3 beats panel 2

▪
The surplus canvas stops being void. Panel 2 letterboxes: Dortmund's L0 is a 199 m corridor, so aspect-fit leaves ~70% of a landscape canvas empty beside it. Panel 3 expands the frame to the canvas aspect instead and fills it with real geometry — same scale, same route size, more station. Nothing is zoomed out to achieve it.
▪
Context is what makes a plan a plan. A blue thread alone can't tell you which side of the hall you're on. The walkway web, the platform slabs, and the other escalators on the floor are all in the export already.
▪
The ghost is the connective tissue — DESIGN.md §7.6's phrase, and prototype.html already drew it as “Platform 4 (above)”. Here it's the real path on the floors this one connects to, dashed. You can see where you'll be standing one tab later.
▪
The scale bar answers the objection in the code. The shipped comment defends the shared bbox with “so the plan never jumps when you switch”. Per-level framing does make the zoom jump — Berlin L0 draws at 6.6 px/m, Dortmund L0 at 0.62. A scale bar makes that legible instead of disorienting, which is what maps have always done.

Paired markers, in the export's own order

▪
path.transitions is emitted in path order and lines up 1:1 with the gaps between level runs — verified on both fixtures. So per floor the marks alternate arrive / leave, and each one knows which it is.
▪
Filled disc = your next move (“Escalator ↑ to L+1”). Hollow ring = where you landed (“in from L0”). One is a decision, one is orientation — today they're the same 24px disc with the same words.
▪
Endpoints are named. “Step off · Pl 11” and “Board · Pl 4” come from path.endpoints, so the first and last floors read as the start and the end rather than as two more level changes.
▪
Radius drops 12 → 9 (out) / 7 (in), and the chips dodge each other. On Dortmund L−1 the two marks are 20.6 m apart — at that floor's own scale they're 85px apart and no longer touch.
04

What the export can't support

Every one of these would make the levels view better and none of them is in the data. They are listed here instead of drawn — the shipped prototype fakes some of them, which is part of why the real thing disappoints when it arrives. Two are cheap contract changes if you want them.

Measured on the fixtures, not recalled: 0 nodes carry ele in Berlin Hbf; step_count is absent on 100% of both fixtures' ways (the whole key is missing from the export schema, not just sparse); Berlin's levels_present carries −1.7 / −1.3 / −0.5 mezzanine landings that round into −2 / −1 / −1; and level_raw holds compound values like "−2;−1" and one honest OSM typo, "0,5".

05

The call

What I'd sign off, in the order I'd build it.

First · ~30 lines

Panel 2

Own-bbox framing, picker from pathLevels (it already exists on WalkScene, the picker just doesn't read it), paired in/out markers, and nodata grey for "vertical". This is the whole “weird and unusable” complaint, and it's a day. It is not the ceiling — but it clears the floor.

Then · the payoff

Panel 3

Aspect-expanded frame + context ways + ghost + scale bar. This is what turns “not broken” into a floor plan you'd actually navigate by. All of it reads the export we already ship. The context is only what the search touched — good enough to be worth it, and better still with the one-line contract change in §04.

Alongside · the card

The ribbon

Different surface, different job — it lives on the transfer card where the plan can't fit, and it kills the “4 level changes — level change + stairs” line. Ship it with the plan fix; they share one pass over runs + transitions. My pick: stepped.

Not proposed

▪
Rotating the plan to the walk's long axis. Tried it, measured it, dropped it. A station-wide principal axis rescues Dortmund L0 (19% → 55% of the canvas) and wrecks L+1 (59% → 28%) — the bridge runs across the concourse. Per-level rotation maximises each floor and destroys the floor-to-floor mental map, which is the one thing the paired markers exist to protect. Aspect-expansion gets the same canvas back without turning anything.
▪
A fix for the 3D tab. Real bug — IsoFit.levelUnit = 20 is a constant while the iso footprint it has to clear is data-dependent (a 100-unit normalised floor projects to 50–71 units of screen-y depending on the orbit angle), so Berlin's floors overlap and the “exploded floors” isn't exploded. The fix is to derive levelUnit from the projected footprint, not to draw stacked plates in CSS — DESIGN.md §7.6 rejects that, and it isn't a prototype question. Filed, not designed.
